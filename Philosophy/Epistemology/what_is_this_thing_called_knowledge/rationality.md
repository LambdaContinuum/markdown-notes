# rationality, justification, and knowledge

a crucial question for epistemologists is to explain the distinction between the rational and irrational. this is important because for those who theorize about knowledge, intuitively, only rational beliefs are candidates of knowledge.

There seems to be a close connection between rationality and justification. It seems plausible to suppose in most cases a justified belief is a rational one and vice versa. Given we've noted that justification is plausibly necessary for knowledge, this close connection between justification and rationality would explain why we tend to regard rationality as necessary for knowledge.

# epistemic rationality and the goal of truth

the kind of rationality we're interested in is epistemic rationality. This is a form of rationality which is aimed at the goal of true belief.

There's a legitimate distinction to be made between epistemic rationality and other forms. An action can be the best way to achieve your goals (and be rational in that sense), but not be conducive to obtaining true beliefs. See any hypothetical were self-deception helps a goal of yours.

# the goals of epistemic rationality

a problem facing the notion of epistemic rationality is that it's hard to see exactly how its concerned with true belief, not just that it merely is concerned.

maybe the most natural way of understanding epistemic rationality is a demand that one should maximize one's true beliefs. There are problems with the maximizing conception of epistemic rationality. This account implies we should try to have as many true beliefs as possible, so there are straightforward counter-examples.

For example. memorizing names and addresses from the phone book may well lead me to have thousands of true beliefs, but the beliefs in question wouldn't be of consequence. Indeed, we would usually regard this sort of truth seeking behavior as irrational (maybe there's equivocatin occuring between epistemic rationality and 'practical' rationality?)

Setting that aside, there's a more fundamental difficulty. The best way to maximize the number of ones true beliefs is to believe just about anything, since this would ensure that one has the most chance of believing the truth. crucially this sort of strategy would lead one to form a lots of false beliefs as well.

We might want to modify our conception of epistemic rationality so that it demands not that one maximizes truth in ones beliefs but rather that one minimizes falsehoods. The problem with this is also obvious: the best way to minimize ones false beliefs is to not believe in anything at all.

We need some way of balancing the goal of maximizing truth in ones beliefs with the related goal of minimizing falsehoods. We want agents to take some risks regarding the falsity of their beliefs, but equally we don't want agents to go all out for the truth at expense of widespread falsity in their beliefs.

Specifying just how to understand this balanced conception is quite hard.

# rationality and responsibility

we implicitly take for granted that agents are in some sense responsible for the truth seeking procedures there's employing. There's good reason for this since typicalyl we only blame or praise agents for doing things where they can reasonably be thought to be responsible for their actions.

it isn't always obvious that agents can be held responsible for the way in which they form beliefs however. One consideration in this respect is that some beliefs such as basic perceptual beliefs are both spontaneous and involuntary and so just dont seem to be the sort of thing that agents can have any control over.

Setting that aside there remains the problem that sometimes agents are simply taught the wrong epistemic norms to follow, where an epistmeic norm is a rule which one follows in order to gain true beliefs.

Imagine someone who has been raised by being taught all the wrong epistemic norms. SUppose she's been taught that one ought to find out the truth about certain matters by flipping a coin. Is this person forming her beliefs in an epistmeically rational way?

One possibility is that we might regard this agent as being entirely epistmeically rational since by her lights she is forming her beliefs in the right kind of way. There is a big difference between someone who forms her belief by tossing a coin who should know better and someone who forms her belief by tossing a coin and who to the best of her knowledge bnelieves that this is the right way to proceed.

In the former case the agent is responsible, and so blameworthy, for the way she forms her belief in a way that isnt applicable to the latter case.

The kind of epistmeic rationality that is at issue here is called a deontic epistmeic rationality. IT holds that an agents belief is epistemically rational just so long as the agent does not contravene any epistemic norm in coming to form that belief by her own lights. That is an agent can be epistmeically rational and employ the wrong epistemic norms just so long as she's not to blame for her employment of the wrong epistemic norms. This is a weak conception of what epistemic rationality demands.

In contrast, as stronger non-deontic conception of epistemic rationality would demand that agents not only do not blamelessy contravene any epistemic norm, but that the epistemic norms in question should be the right ones (i.e. the truth conducive ones). On the non-deontic view even the agent who blamelessly cforms her belief by tossing a coin does not count as epistemically rational. The problem with this thesis is it seems to break the intuitive connection between epistmeic rationality and responsibility. On this view one can be respnosible and yet not at the same time be epistemically rational.

# epistemic internalism/externalism

we are caught between two opposing conceptions of epistemic rationality. the first has the advantage of directly connecting the notino of epistemic rationality with that of responsibility, but has the drawback that the demands it imposes are very week.

The second imposes a stronger constraint on epistemic rationality but does so at the expense of breaking the link between epistemic rationality and repsonsibility.

We might call the former deontic notion of epistemic rationality an epistemic internalist conception of epistemic rationality in that it ties epistemic rationality to what the agent can be held accountable for.

we might call the latter notion of rationality an epistemic externality conception of epistemic rationality in that it breaks the connection between epistemic rationality and what the agent can be held accountable for.

Roughly speaking epistemic internalism makes ones epistemic standing something that one has control over; epistemic externalism allows that one's epistemic standing can sometimes depend on factors outside of ones control (e.g. whether one has been taught the right epistemic norms).

There is a general philosophical issue here whic has ramifications for epistemology as a whole. The problem is that the best way in which to get to the truth need not be discernible to the agent themself. Should our epistemiology be ego-centic and therefore focused on what the agent is able to discern (i.e. what the agent has good reason to believe are the correct epistemic norms, whether or not they really are the correct epistmeic norms); or whether it should be non-egocentric in the sense that it allows that other considerations can play a role in determining whether or not an agents belief is epistmeically raitonally held (e.g. whether the epistemic norms that the agent is using are, in fact, the right ones)

Its easy to see how this relates to epistemology as a whole when we consider how rationality relates to concepts like justification and knowledge.

Prima facie there ought to be a close connection between justificaiton and epistemic rationality since we often use terms like 'rational' and 'justified' as roughly synonymous. With this in mind we might argue that justification is just epistemic rationality. If one held that epistmeic rationality is just deontic epistemic rationality, then one would end up with an epistemic internalist egocentric conception of justification. The problem with this proposal is that it would have the consequence that justified true belief and knowledge could come radically apart.

Remember our coin tossing agent who is deontically epistmically rational. It's hard to see how he could know anything on the basis of a coin flip, since even if she does end up with a true belief by employing the wrong epistemic norm it would merely be a matter of luck that her belief is true. We don't normally treat agents who get to the truth via luck as knowers. In this view of the relationship between justification and epistemic rationality however the agent concerned would be entirely justified in forming her beliefs this ay.

This may look like another Gettier case where one has JTB but not knowledge and so one might think the problem at hand is a variant of the familiar problem posed by Gettier cases. Notice, however, that the case jsut described is importantly different from normal Gettier cases since in Gettier cases the agent typically forms her belief via the correct epistemic norm, its just that the truth of belief is nonetheless lucky. In this case the agent is employing the wrong epistemic norm, albeit blamelessly. Given this difference between these two cases, the divorce between JTB and knowlednge opened up by this conception of epistemic rationality and therefore justification may remain even if we've found a way to deal with Gettier cases.

Another reason to think a conception of jsutification in terms of deontic epistmeic rationality pulls justification apart from knowledge is that we often ascribe knowledge to agents even when they don't form their beliefs responsibly, just so long as they form their beliefs in the right kind of way. So it seems that deontic epistemic rationality cannot be necessary for knowledge, since this form of rationality entails the belief in question was responsibily forms and yet knowledge doesnt seem to require this.

For example, consider a child that may go about forming a perceptual belief by simply believing what she seems to see. SUppose that circumstances are otherwise normal and there is nothing to indicate that her senses should be doubted in this case . Wouldn't we say that such a believe is an instance of knowledge, that the child knows that there is a toy in front of her? The problem is that we would hardly regard the child as believing responsibly, since she isn't in fact paying attention to how she forms her beliefs at all. She's simply doing what comes naturally to her entirely unreflectively. Nevertheless forming your beliefs in this way is generally reliable as a way to form ones beliefs about the world. Moreover it doesnt seemt o contravene any epistmeic norms. So even while not believing responsibly one can gain knowledge and this seems to suggest that knowing does not require deontic epistemic rationality.

you might then want to characterize justification in terms of non-deontic epstemic raitonaltiy. This faces the difficulty of our everyday notion of epistmic raitonality being closely associated with the notion of responsibility. We would normally regard an agent as epistemically rational if she responsibly formed her belief via the blameless use of the wrong epistmeic norms.

There's no straightforward way of reconciling these conflicting intuitions about the relationship between epistemic rationality, justification, repsonsibility, and knowledge. Much contemporary epistmeic theorizing has been concerned with offering different pictures of how these concepts relate. This conflcit of intuitions has led some to argue that we should treat epistmeic rationality and knowledge as different notions.

The idea is that justification is essentialy epistemic rationality and that epistemic rationality is just deontic epistemic rationality and thus we should simply accept theres no direct conneciton between knowledge and justification.

we replace the traditional conception of epistemology with seeks an integrated account of these three notions with one that regards epistemology as concerned with two distinct projects:

1. to analyze those epistmeics concepts that are closely tied to responsibility: epistmeic rationality and justification
2. to analyze knowlednge

the problems we've explored here should give us pause to take this suggestion seriously.
