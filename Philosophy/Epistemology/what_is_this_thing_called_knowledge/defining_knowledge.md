# the problem of the criterion

an initial problem arises to one who wants to offer a definition of knowlege. Where to begin. You might initially start by looking at the cases in which one has knowledge and considering what is common to each case.

If one doesn't already know what knowledge is, how can one correctly identify cases of knowledge in the first place? This is the problem of the criterion. It consists of two claims:

1. I can only identify instances of knowledge provided I already know what the criteria for knowledge are.
2. I can only know what the criteria for knowledge are provided I am able to identify instances of knowledge.

This is unpleasant, you must either assume you can independently know what the criteria for knowledge are in order to identify instances of knowledge, or you must assume you can identify instances of knowledge in order to determine what the criteria for knowledge are. Either move seems dubious and may threaten the legitimacy of defining knowledge.

# methodism and particularism

this problem dates to antiquity, but contemporary focus is due to Roderick Chisholm. He noted, historically, philosophers tended to begin by assuming that they already know, or are able to identify through philosophical reflection alone, the criteria for knowlewdge, and have proceeded on this basis to examine the issue of whether we have any knowledge. Chisholm calls this stance methodism. Descartes is a famous example he cites.

The contrast, and what Chisholm argues we should adopt, is the other horn of the dilemma. What he calls particularism. THe particularist instead assumes one can correctly identify particular instances of knowledge and proceed on this basis to determine the criteria for knowledge.

One of the main advantages (?) for methodism is it doesn't begin by assuming the falsity of skepticism since it elaves na open question whether there is anything that meets the criteria for knowledge. The big problem facing this view is it seems totally mysterious how we are to get a grip on the criteria for knowledge without appealing to particular instances of knowledge.

Persuaded by this most epistemologists have followed Chisholm in adopting particularism. In favor of particularlism is the thought that if one has to assume anything in this regard, it's less extravagant to suppose we can correctly identify particular cases of knowledge independently of any prior awareness of what the criteria for knowledge are then to suppose we can identify what the criteria for knowledge are without appeal to cases of knowledge. Those sympathetic to skepticism will baulk at the particularist methodology since they'll argue that the claim we do indeed possess knowledge is something that has to be shown.

The problem of the criterion wouldn't be so pressing if the criteria for knowledge was entirely obvious, since if they were the assumption key to methodism, that we can know the criteria for knowledge independently of examining any particular instances of knowledge (by simply reflecting on the concept of knowledge for example) would not be nearly so implausible. The difficulty is that reflection itself indicates there's no simple account of the criteria for knowledge available.

# knowledge as justified true belief

What more is there to knowledge than true belief? A natural answer, often ascribed to Plato, is justification for ones belief. This proposal is known as the classical account of knowledge.

# Gettier cases

The classical theory of knowledge has been shown to be completely untenable. Gettier showed that you could have justified true belief and yet lack knowledge of what you believed because your true belief was ultimately a matter of luck.

Imagine a man named John who comes downstairs one morning and sees that the time on the grandfather clock in the hall says '8:20'. On this basis John comes to believe that it is 8:20 a.m., and this belief is true since it is 8:20 a.m. Morever John's belief is justified in that its based on excellent grounds. For example, John usually comes downstairs in the morning about this time, so he knows that the time is about right. MOrever, this clock has been very reliable at telling the time for many years and John has no reason to think it's faulty now. So he has good reasons for thinking that the time on the clock is correct.

Suppose, however, that the clock had stopped twenty four hours earlier so John is now forming his justified true belief by looking at a stopped clock. Intuitively, if this were so, John would lack knowledge even though he has met the conditions laid down by the classical account of knowledge. After all, that John has a true belief in this case is ultimately a matter of luck. If John had come downstairs a moment earlier or a moment later then he would have formed a false belief about the time. (Plato likens knowledge to the Statues of Daedalus, the point of knowledge is it should be harder to get wrong than true belief)

The general form to all Gettier cases is as follows: We need to note that you can have a justified false belief, since this is crucial for Gettier cases. With this in mind, there are three stages to constructing your own Gettier case.

1. Take an agent who forms her belief in a way that would usually lead her to have a false belief.
2. You add some det ail to the example to ensure that the agent's belief is justified nonetheless. In the example above, the detail we added was the agent had no reason for thinking the clock wasn't working properly, given it's normally reliable.
3. You make the case such that while the way in which the agent formed her belief would normally have resulted in a justified false belief, in the case it so happened that the belief was true.

# responding to the gettier cases

There's no easy way to respond to the Gettier cases. Initially, it was thought that all one needed to do was simply tweak the classical account of knowledge. One such proposal was that in order to have knowledge, one's true belief must be justified and also not in any way based on false presupposiitons (like the clock is working). There's a devastating problem to this sort of proposal, it's difficult to spell out this idea of a presupposition such that it's strong enough to deal with Gettier cases and yet not so strong that it prevents us from having most of the knowledge that we think we have.

It's also not clear that the agent in a Gettier case need pressupose anything at all. Consider an example Chisholm gives. We have a farmer, called Gayle, who forms her belief that there is a sheep in the field by looking at a shaggy dog which happens to look just like a sheep. As it turns out, there is a sheep in the field standing behind the dog and hence Gayle's belief is true. Moreover her belief is also justified because she has great evidence for thinking that there is a sheep in the field.

Given the immediacy of Gayle's belief in this case it's hard to see that it really presupposes any further beliefs at all unless we are to udnerstand the notion of presupposition very liberally. If we do understand the notion in such a way that Gayle counts as illictly making a presupposition the problem then reemerges of how to account for apparently genuine cases of knowledge.

Once it was recognized there was no easy answer to the problem posed by gettier cases the race was on to find a radically new way of analyzing knowledge which was Gettier proof. A feature they all share is they understand the conditions for knowledge such that they demand more in the way of cooperation from the world than simply that the belief in question is true. 

On the classical account there is one condition which relates to the world, the truth condition, and two conditions that relate to the agents, the belief and justification conditions. These last two conditions, don't demand anything from the world in the sense that they could obtain regardless of how the world is. The moral of the Gettier cases is you need to demand more from the world than simply that ones justified belief is true.

The new analyses will be discussed later.

# back to the problem of the criterion

the criteria for knowledge are far from obvious, and this calls into question the idea that we could determine such criteria without making reference to actual cases of knowledge. However, if its really the case that knowledge is such a complicated knowledge then how can it be that we are able to identify cases of knowledge correctly even while lacking a prior grasp of what the criteria for knowledge are? From the start of this epistmeic project we are faced with a deep and seemingly intractable puzzle.
