Russell's target is the view known as "Linguistic Doctrine of Necessary Truth" (L-DONT) sometimes called "Conventionalism about Necessity".

It's the view that necessity is grounded in the meanings of our expressions, meanings which are sometimes identified with conventions governing those expressions, and that knowledge of that necessity is based on our knowledge of those meanings or conventions.

The simplest form of the view states that a truth, if it's necessary, is necessary (and knowably necessary) because it's analytic.

It's widely recognized this simple version of the view faces a prima facie problem with the existence of the necessary a posteriori. If all analytic truths are a priori, if there are necessary a posteriori truths then there are necessary synthetic truths, contradicting the views claim that all necessary truths are analytic. 

Contemporary L-DONTers have responses, but Russell wants to suggest there is a different more serious problem that arises from indexicality.

Consider Kaplan's sentence:

- (1) I am here now.

Kaplan argues, relative to a context in which I am the speaker, this sentence expresses a contingent truth. (I didn't have to be here now) but this needn't be the case. Suppose that God were the speaker and that God was necessarily everywhere at all times. Relative to such a context the sentence would express a necessary proposition.

The problem for L-DONT is that of how we are to explain the necessity in the second context. It can't be explained by the meanings of the words in the sentence, since know from the first context that those meanings are insufficient to produce necessity.

# Background
---

L-DONT is typically associated with the positivists, particularly Carnap, and it's a vew that boasts genuine virtues. It offers a metaphysics and epistemology of necessity, certaintly the de dicto variety and on some versions the nontrivial de re kind as well, and to provide these things without positing anything so mysterious as possibilia, essences, or a faculty of intuition. Given the ubiquity of necessity talk this is an attractive offer.

Quine sharing the positivists ontological parsimony and empirical mindedness felt the draw of the view but he held that the linguistic attempt to explain necessity was bound to fail. He argued it was impossible to explain de re necessity in terms of de dicto necessity and that there were no analytic sentences to ground de dicto necessity anyways.

There was a move away from strict metaphysical parsimony in the late 20th century, and it made space for a move towards analyzing meaning in terms of necessity as opposed to necessity in terms of meaning. In Lewis's "General Semantics" for example, analyticity is analyzed in terms of necessity iwth necessity itself analyzed in terms of concrete possible worlds. More recently Kit Fine has argued for the existence of essences, where essences are not reducible to modal phenomena such as de re necessity, and then used essences to explain analyticity.

The linguistic doctrine still speaks to philosophers today however: if anything the work of contemporary metaphysicians by demonstrating the fruitfulness of modal concepts, has made many of us hungries for an explanation of the nature of and epistemology of modality. There have been explicit attempts to revive LDONT, in the works of Alan Sidelle and Amie Thomasson for example.

The authors recognize the philosophhical study of language and modal phenomena has undergone a massive growth since the days of Carnap and Quine. Contemporary L-DONTers have to be sensitive to three things that the work of earlier writers often ignored.

1. One must distinguish between sentences and what the sentences say (more technically, the propositions they express). to things that were often run together in the writings of the positivists. It is sentences that have meanings, the propositions they express that are necessary or contingent. 
2. One must distinguish, conceptually, between analyticity, a priority, necessity, and logical truth. To show that a sentence is analytic is not already to succeed in showing that it expresses a necessary proposition.
3. It is usually recognized that one must say something sensible about the necessary a posteriori. If there are necessary a posteriori truths, then either supports of the linguistic doctrine have to accept that there are analytic truths which are a posteriori or one must back off from the claim that all necessary truthsare analytic to a weaker claim such as that all necessary truths have their modal status as a result of our linguistic conventions. They may also deny the existence of the necessary a posteriori but the versions of L-DONT russell will examine are not intended to have rtadical revisionary consequences for our views about which things are necessary.

# The Necessary A Posteriori
---

The necessary a posteriori has been with us since Kripke argued that both ordinary identity sentences using names, like:

- (2) Hesperus is Phosphorus

and theoretical identity sentences like

- (3) Heat is mean molecular motion
- (4) Lightning is electricity

express necessary truths, even though they seem to be things that we had to discover empirically and hence a posteriori. Consider the following valid argument:

P1. Some sentences express propositions which are necessary and not a priori.
P2. All analytic sentences express a priori propositions.
C. Some sentences express necessary propositions and are not analytic.  

Since the fact that a nonanalytic sentence expresses a necessary truth can not be explained by the sentence's being analytic, it follows that the fact that a sentence expresses a necessary truth is not always explained by the fact that it's analytic.

More concretely, consider these sentences each of which is plausibly synthetic but expresses a necessary truth:

- (5) Hesperus is Phosphorus
- (6) Hesperus is not Mars
- (7) Water is H2O

(5) is the most famous example of the necessary a posteriori, but its status as such is susceptible to challenge. Some Millians about names consider it to express an a priori proposition, one the grounds that it expresses the same proposition as the sentence 'Hesperus is Hesperus', which is knowable a priori. A Millian might claim that the sentence is true in virtue of its meaning: on the Millian conception of the meaning of names, the meaning of 'Hesperus' is the same as the meaning of 'Phosphorus'. This alongside with the meaning of the identity symbol allows one to hold that (5) is necessary and analytic after all.

Russell isn't incliend to think (5) is analytic but even if it were, it's sufficient to observe that the Millian argument is not available in all cases. One kind of sentence resistant to the above problem is that of nonidentities between names such as (6).

It's implausible that someone could know a priori that Mars was not Hesperus. Russell has once heard that (6) might be analytic. The thought was that if 'Hesperus' and 'Mars' are both Millian names, then their meanings are simply their referents. For 'Hesperus is not Mars' to be true, all that is requires is that 'Hesperus' and 'mars' have different Milliean meanings. Given the meanings of all the expressions in the sentence, the sentence has to be true.

Be that as it may - and if 'Hesperus is not Mars' turns out to be analytic we seem to have comea long way from our old conception of analyticity - our third sentence (7) is definetly not analytic in the same way. 'Water' is a semantically simple natural kind term, whereas 'H2O' is a semantically complex expression for the same natural kind; it contains the meanings of 'hydrogen' and 'oxygen' and '2' as parts. Since no content can be both simple and complex, 'water' and 'h2o' have different meanings and hence there's no plausible argument that (7) is analytic. We have a genuine example of the necessary synthetic.

# The Sidelle Stratagem
---

Sidelle suggests we abandon the idea that all necessary truths must be expressed by analytic sentences, and instead require only that the necessity of the truth be somehow explained by our linguistic conventions.

Sidelle's version of thhe linguistic doctrine, according to which meanings are the source of modality retain nearly all the virtues of the simpler stronger doctrine; model phenomena still receive a metaphysical explanation in terms of more ontologically pedestrian linguistic phenomena and our knowledge of modal truths is still grounded in our linguistic knowledge.

But how can our linguistic conventions ground the necessity of synthetic sentences like (5)-(7)? Sidelle's idea is that some very general principles, which he calls 'principles of individuation' are analytic. As an example he suggests:

\[IP\] forall x (if x belongs to kind K, then if p is x's P-property, then it is necessary that x is p)

One way to get an intuitive grip on this very general principle is by considering some isntances of it such as:

\[W1\] if water belongs to kind K then if p is water's P-property, then it is necessary that water is p.

\[W2\] if water belongs to kind K then if H2O is water's P-property, then it is necessary that water is H2O.

\[W3\] if water is a chemical kind, then if H2O is water's chemical structure, then it is necessary that water is H2O.

Suppose we thought that sentence 'water is a chemical kind' was analytic. Then using only analytic principles and the familiar logical rules of universal insantiation and modus ponens, we can derive the sentence:

\[W4\] If H2O is water's chemical structure, then it is necessary that water is H2O.

A claim that our necessary synthetic sentence (7) is indeed necessary can be derived from two premises:

\[W4\] If H2O is water's chemical structure, then it is necessary that water is H2O.
\[D\] H2O is water's chemical structure
\[N1c\] It is necessary that water is H2O.

Premise (D) ('for discovery') is, as Sidelle admits, a statement of an empirically discovered a posteriori contingent fact. But now consider something that was at least one point an epistemic possibility: suppose 'water' had denoted XYZ. Consider this argument:

\[W4\] If XYZ is water's chemical structure, then it is necessary that water is XYZ.
\[D\] XYZ is water's chemical structure
\[N1c\] It is necessary that water is XYZ

The conclusion is different but its still a claim that it's necessary that water have a particular chemical structure and Sidelle's claim is that this is because the necessity of it comes from the necessity (and ultimately the analyticity) of the principles from which we derived (W4) and not from the empirical discovery (D).

He writes: "We are now in a position to see how the necessary a posteriori could be suceptible to a conventionalist account. Suppose that general principles of individuation are analytic....Then it will be a matter of convention, say, that if something is a chemical kind, then it has its chemical microstructure necessarily. Thus, if we can add the ostensibly non-modal 'Water is a chemical  kind' and 'The microstructure of water is (actually) H2O' we will be able to derive that water is H2O, and all the modal force of this conclusion will be derived from our general principle, which we are supposing to be analytic.

Whether Sidelle's solution to the problem of the necessary a posteriori survives Yablo's Quinean attack in his review of the book is controversial. But writers such as Chalmers, Ted Sider, and Amie THomasson have received the idea sympathetically. Russell believes it has been a mistake to focus on the necessity a psoterio as the main obstacle to a linguistic account of necessity.

# Indexicality
---

An indexical expression is an expression whose content is non-trivially determined by the context in which it's uttered. Classical exmaples include pure indexicals like "I", and "now", demosntrative expressions like "this" and "that" and adjectives like "tall" and "heavy".

More controversially philosophers have argued other expressions, such as the vague predicates 'red' and 'heap', the truth predicate, and 'knows that' are indeixcal. Despite the controversies about the extent of indexicality within natural languages, it's safe to say that the view that there are some indexical expressions is uncontroversial.

We need to appreciate two consequences of the presence of indexicals in a language.
1. Indexicals make it possible for the sentences containing them to express different propositions relative to different contexts.
2. Indexicals make conceptual space for the idea of a special kind of sentence: one that expresses different propositions relative to different contexts, but will nevertheless express a true proposition relative to every context.

The second consequence isn't required for the argument to go through, but some of the clearest illustrations of the basic argument make use of it.

Consider (1), this is because the content, the contribution the expression makes to the proposition expressed by the sentence containing it, varies with context and the propoisition expressed by a sentence is determined by the content of its component parts. For example, the sentence "I like tofu", when uttered in a context in which Russell is the speaker, expresses the proposition Gillian Russell likes tofu, but when uttered in the context in which the current speaker is Ban Ki Moon expresses a different proposition, namely that Ban Ki Moon lieks tofu.

Since sentences express different propositions given different contexts, and propositions are often taken to be the meanings of sentences, it seems clear that sentences must have two kinds of meaning.: 

The first is something that stays constant and is used to determine which proposition is expressed in a given context, Roughly speaking it's a function from context to content expressed. This is the disambiguation of our folk word "meaning" that is commonly, following Kaplan, called "character". 

The other kind of meaning is the thing that varies with context, the content. In the case of a sentence it's the proposition expressed, and determines a function from circumstance of evaluation to truth value.

The kind of meaning LDONT is appealing to in attempting to explain necessity, and knowledge of necessity in terms of meaning and knowledge of meaning is character. This is because it is character, not contents, that speakers learn to associate with expressions when they learn a language. Sentences containing indexicals don't really have content of their own, except relative to contexts, and the contents they have relative to contexts can be expressed by others, apparently non-synonymous expressions.

If we borrow from Kaplan's formal logic LD and take contexts to be quadruples of an agent (the speaker), a, a day, d, a place, p, and a possible world w: <a, d, p, w>. The expression "I" and "today" will be taken to pick out the agent and the day of the context respectively. Suppose we have two contexts, c1 and c2. In c1 Russell is the agent, the day is July 24th 2008, the palce is Melbourne University, and the possible world is the actual world. In c2, the agent is Ban Ki moon the day is July 25 2008, the place is Seoul National University, and the world is still the actual world. Now consider the sentence "I want to eat Tofu today". Relative to c1 it expresses the proposition that Gillian Russell wants to eat tofu on July 24th 2008. Relative to c2 it expresses the proposition that Mr. Ban wants to eat tofu on July 25th 2008.

Now consider (2).

\[Y\] Yesterday came before today.

With respect to c1, Y says that 23rd July 2008 came before 24th July 2008, but with respect to c2 it says that 24th July 2008 comes before 25th July 2008. Yet there will be no context in which it fails to express a true proposition. Consider Kaplan's sentence:

\[L\]I am here now.

What Kaplan thought interesting about this sentence was it seemed to be analytic as its meaning guarantees its truth and hence a priori even though it usually expresses a contingent proposition; L expresses a true proposition regardless of the context in which it's uttered.

It seems clear that the intent behind the idea that the modal status of a proposition is to be explained by the analyticity of a sentence that expresses it is to appeal to a feature of the sentence that is constant from context to context. We have no space for the idea of a sentence that is analytic in some contexts and not in others.


# The Basic Problem
---

Here's the basic problem indexicals pose for L-DONT. Indexicals allow a single sentence to express different propositions in different contexts, and since modal status is a property of propositions, this means the same sentence can express propositions with different modal statuses, given different contexts. It's hard to see how this can be compatible with the modal status of the proposition expressed being something that is a consequence of the meaning of the sentence.

Consider "I am here now". This sentence expresses a contingent proposition whenever you utter it, but it is not impossible for "I am here now" to express a necessary proposition. SUppose, that God necessarily exists and that he is necessarily in all places at all times, he is omnipresent. Then in a context <a, p, d, w> in which a is God, and p, d, and w are any place, day and possible world, the sentence "I am here now" expresses necessary proposition.

It is much more common for the sentence to express a contingent truth. So 'I am here now' is a sentence that expresses necessary truths with respect to some contexts and contingent truths with respect to others. The necessity in the former contexts can hardly be explained by the meaning of the sentence, or by conventions governing the words in the sentence. If the meaning of the sentence were sufficient for it to express necessary truths, it would express a necessary truth regardless of the context.

Also consider the sentence (8) I exist. This sentence expresses a contingent truth when the agent of the context is Russell but a necessary truth when the agent is any necessary existence such as God.

Consider (9) That can be halved.

The sentence expresses a merely contingent truth when the demonstration provided by the context indicates a cake, there are possible worlds in which the cakes are the basic building block of the universe and with respect to those worlds the proposition will be false. But with respect a context in which the object demonstrated is the number 6, 'that can be halved' is a necessary truth.

(10) Fthat exists.

Let's introduce a new indexical to our language. 'Fthat' directly refers to whatever object the speaker's favorite thing is in the world of the context at the time of the context. If Ban Ki Moon's favorite thing is the number 4 and he utters the sentence then the sentence expresses a proposition which is necessary, the same proposition that is expressed in all contexts by '4 exists' presuming '4' is a directly referential name. However, if my favorite thing is Bob and I utter 'fthat exists', then the sentence expresses a contingent proposition, namely the one that is always expressed by the non-indexical sentence 'Bob exists'.

# The Underlying Issue
---

The underlying problem, which indexicality serves to bring out, is that necessity is a property of propositions, whereas analyticity and logical truth are properties of sentences.

That logical truth is a property of sentences can be seen by considering indexical logics like LD, in which "I am here now" is a logical truth despite expressing a variety of different propositions. Thus the sentence cannot have inherited its status as a logical truth from the proposition it expresses.

There are a number of considerstaions that show analyticity is a property of sentences.  For one 'analytic' is taken as synoyonm for truth in virtue of meaning. The intended interpretation of this is true in virtue of its meaning. This means that the kinds of things that are analytic must be the kinds of things that have meanings. But propositions don't have meanings, rather they are the meanings of sentences.

Secondly, we normally attribute necessity and a priority (both properties of propositions) with that-clauses, i.e. it's necessary that or it's a priori that. but this locution is less natural with  analyticity, it's more natural to use a metalinguistic construction such as "'All bachelors are unmarried' is analytic".

Third, the sentence "I am here now" is plausibly analytic in the sense that something about the meanings of the terms involved guarantees that the sentence must be true. But the propositions expressed need not have any special status; it might simply be the proposition that Gillian Russell is in her office at 15:19. So where the sentence and proposition may come apart it seems that the analyticity remains with the sentence, suggesting sentences are the primary bearers of the property.

Finally, we must consider the natural affinity between analyticity and logical truth along with the fact that logical truth is a property of sentences.

The fact that analyticity and necessity have different primary bearers is not sufficient to show that necessity is not explained by analyticity. It could have been the case that a proposition was necessary just in case it was expressed by an analytic sentence or just in case it was related to an analytic sentence by an appropriate Sidelle-type derivation. But when we couiple the fact that analyticity and necessity have different bearers with the fact that indexicality allows these bearers to come apart, in that it allows the same sentence to express different propositions, we have the perfect situation to show that the two properties aren't so related.

# Conclusion
---

There was a time where analytic truth and necessary truth we so closely identified that one could call something "analytic" and know that one's audience would take that to mean a priori, logical, linguistic, and of course necessary.

Since then philosophy of language has carved wedge after wedge to put between these notions: the insinstence on a carefully maintained sentence/proposition distinction, the contingent analytic, and the necessary a posteriori.

Serious attempts have been made to hold together more sophisticated versions of L-DONT in the face of this split but Russell has argued that one of the better tools for seperating necessity and analyticity, indexicality, has been underestimated.
